## SCRIPT STRUCTURE

### SCENE 1 — THE HOOK (0:00–0:15)
**Layout**: FULLSCREEN AVATAR. Business casual attire (collared shirt or blazer). Warm home-office background with bookshelf. Slight lean forward — conversational posture.
**HeyGen Emotion**: Engaged, direct eye contact. Slight head shake on pain-point mention.
**HeyGen Gesture**: None — stillness creates focus on words.

Open with ONE of these hook patterns (rotate across videos, never repeat within a 10-video window):

- **The Mistake Hook**: "I wasted [specific timeframe] doing [specific thing wrong] before I figured out [insight]. Let me save you that headache."
- **The Contrarian Hook**: "Everyone says [common advice]. That's wrong — or at least, it's incomplete. Here's what actually works."
- **The Proof Hook**: "[Specific result or number]. That's what happened after [specific action]. Here's exactly how."
- **The Nightmare Hook**: "Picture this. You're paying [dollar amount] a month for [list of separate tools], none of them talk to each other, and your leads are falling through the cracks. Sound familiar?"
- **The Pattern-Interrupt Hook**: Start mid-thought, mid-story, or with a surprising statement that doesn't resolve for 10+ seconds.

RULES FOR THE HOOK:
- Start with something concrete: a name, a number, a date, a place. Never an abstraction.
- Say the target keyword "{{target_keyword}}" within the first 12 seconds.
- Create an open loop — pose a question or start a story you won't finish until later.
- Maximum 3 sentences. Punchy. No warmup.

### SCENE 2 — CREDIBILITY + ROADMAP (0:15–0:45)
**Layout**: FULLSCREEN AVATAR.
**HeyGen Emotion**: Confident, warm. Slight smile on the roadmap.
**HeyGen Gesture**: Subtle nod when listing what they'll learn. Brief hand raise on "subscribe."

- One sentence establishing why the viewer should listen. Reference something specific to this topic — a client outcome, a number of times you've built this exact workflow, or a specific pain point you've solved.
- Roadmap: Tell them exactly what they'll walk away with. "By the end of this, you'll know [specific outcome one], [specific outcome two], and [the thing that trips up ninety percent of people doing this for the first time]."

### SCENES 3 through N — THE BODY (0:45 to [video_length minus 1:30])
**Layout**: PICTURE-IN-PICTURE. Avatar in lower-right corner at ~20% frame size. Main screen shows software dashboard, screen recordings, annotated screenshots, or diagrams. Avatar goes FULLSCREEN only at the start of each new section (10-15 seconds) to re-establish personal connection, then shrinks back to PiP.

Split into {{num_sections}} sections based on the content. Each section follows this micro-structure:

**C) The Takeaway — FULLSCREEN AVATAR (5-10 seconds)**

**RETENTION HOOKS — place one every 60-90 seconds, naturally within the flow:**

## FORMATTING RULES FOR HEYGEN TTS

The script will be fed directly to HeyGen's text-to-speech engine (ElevenLabs). Follow these rules exactly:

**Pacing and Punctuation:**
- Target 150 words per minute at 1.1x playback speed. A 10-minute video ≈ 1,500 words of voiceover.
- Use commas for micro-pauses (0.3 seconds). Periods for standard pauses (0.6 seconds).
- Use "..." (ellipsis) for dramatic beats (1+ seconds). Maximum 4 per script. Place them before reveals or after setup lines.
- After a key insight or important number, insert a period to create a beat: "That's a forty-two percent increase. Let that sink in for a second."
- Break any sentence over 18 words into two sentences. No exceptions.

**Pronunciation and Readability:**
- Write ALL numbers as words: "twenty-five dollars" not "$25", "three hundred" not "300"
- Spell out acronyms on first use: "CRM, or customer relationship management"
- Phonetic guides for terms TTS may butcher:
  - "Ahrefs" → write "Ahrefs (say: 'ay-refs')" on first use, then just "Ahrefs"
  - "GoHighLevel" → write as "Go High Level" (three separate words) so TTS paces it correctly
  - "SaaS" → write "sass" for TTS, note "(SaaS)" in visual direction column
  - "GHL" → write "G-H-L" with hyphens so TTS spells it out
  - "SMTP" → write "S-M-T-P"
  - "Twilio" → write "Twilio (say: 'twil-ee-oh')" on first use
- Add any topic-specific proper nouns to a PRONUNCIATION NOTE at the top of the script output
- Always use contractions: "don't" "you're" "it's" "I've" "that's" "here's" "won't" "can't" "shouldn't"
- Write "percent" not "%"
- Write URLs conversationally: "check the link below" — never spell out a URL

**Emotion and Gesture Markers:**
Include these inline in the Visual Direction column so HeyGen's Avatar IV engine can map appropriate micro-expressions:
- `[EMOTION: frustrated]` — slight brow furrow, head tilt (use during "nightmare" segments)
- `[EMOTION: enthusiastic]` — eyes widen slightly, lean forward (use during reveals and quick wins)
- `[EMOTION: thoughtful]` — slight pause, head tilt, slower pace (use during caveats and nuanced points)
- `[EMOTION: direct]` — square to camera, steady gaze (use during key takeaways)
- `[GESTURE: point-to-screen]` — avatar points toward main screen area (use when referencing a specific UI element)
- `[GESTURE: nod]` — affirmative nod (use when confirming a step works)
- `[GESTURE: palm-up]` — open explaining gesture (use during conceptual explanations)
- `[GESTURE: count]` — counting on fingers (use when listing items)

## ANTI-AI-DETECTION RULES

These are non-negotiable. Violating any of these makes the script sound synthetic and risks YouTube's Inauthentic Content policy:

**1. NO FAKE SPECIFICITY**
- NEVER invent statistics. Every number needs a named source OR is framed as personal experience.
- BAD: "Studies show that seventy-three percent of marketers struggle with automation."
- GOOD: "HubSpot's State of Marketing report found that most marketers still aren't using automation well."
- GOOD: "In my experience, maybe one in four agencies I talk to actually has their automations dialed in."

**2. NO CLICHÉ HOOKS OR FILLER PHRASES**
BANNED — never use any of these, ever:
- "In today's video"
- "What if I told you"
- "Have you ever wondered"
- "Without further ado"
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's dive right in" / "Let's get into it"
- "In this comprehensive guide"
- "Whether you're a beginner or expert"
- "Let's break it down"
- "In the ever-evolving world of"
- "Game-changer" / "game-changing"
- "Revolutionize" / "revolutionary"
- "Unlock the power of" / "unlock the potential"
- "Take it to the next level"
- "At the end of the day"
- "It's important to note that"
- "This is where it gets interesting" (unless preceded by something genuinely interesting)
- "Here's the thing"
- "The reality is"
- "Needless to say"
- "It goes without saying"
- "Let me be clear"
- "The bottom line is"
- "First and foremost"
- "Last but not least"
- "With that being said"
- "Moving on"
- "Now, without wasting any more time"
- "So guys"
- "Alright guys"

**3. VARY SENTENCE RHYTHM — THE MOST IMPORTANT RULE**
- Never write more than 2 sentences of the same length in a row.
- Pattern to follow: Long setup. Short punch. Medium elaboration. Fragment. Then another long one that brings it all together with a specific example.
- Example of correct rhythm:
  "So I had this client — roofing company in Tampa — who was spending four hours a day manually following up with leads. Four hours. Every single day. And when I showed her what a properly built Go High Level workflow could do, she literally didn't believe me until I screen-shared it. Her follow-up time dropped to about twelve minutes."

**4. NO FILLER SENTENCES**
- Every sentence must: teach something, advance the narrative, build credibility, or create anticipation.
- If a sentence exists only to transition, make it do double duty:
  "OK so that covers the trigger setup — but triggers without the right conditions are basically a leaky bucket, and conditions are where I see most people mess up."

**5. USE REAL NAMES, REAL TOOLS, REAL MENU PATHS**
- "Click on Automation, then Workflows, then Create New Workflow" — not "navigate to the automation section"
- "I tested this in HubSpot, ActiveCampaign, and Go High Level" — not "I tested this in several CRM platforms"
- Name real features: "Go High Level's Workflow Builder" not "the platform's automation feature"
- Name real competitors when comparing: "Mailchimp charges you forty-nine bucks a month just for email. Calendly is another fifteen. Yext is like thirty-five for listings."

**6. OPEN LOOPS — MINIMUM 3 PER SCRIPT**
- Plant a question or tease early that you don't resolve until later.
- Track every loop. Every one opened MUST be closed before the final scene.
- Example: "Now there's a reason I set it up this way instead of the obvious approach, and I'll show you exactly why in about two minutes — but first..."
- Example: "And wait till you see what happens when a lead doesn't respond. That part's actually kind of genius."

**7. INCLUDE IMPERFECTION — MINIMUM 2 PER SCRIPT**
Real experts share mistakes, uncertainty, caveats, and strong opinions. Include at least 2 of these per script:
- **A past mistake**: "I used to set this up with a simple timer delay and it caused so many problems. Took me embarrassingly long to switch to the 'Wait for Contact Event' action."
- **An honest caveat**: "Now, this doesn't work great for everyone. If your clients are in healthcare and need HIPAA compliance, you'll want to configure this part differently."
- **A strong opinion**: "Honestly, I think most people overcomplicate this. You don't need fifteen steps in this workflow. Five will do."
- **Self-correction mid-thought**: "Actually, let me back up — I want to explain that differently because the way I just said it makes it sound harder than it is."
- **Colloquial aside**: "Look, I know that sounds basic. But you'd be surprised how many agencies skip this step and then wonder why their funnel has a thirty percent drop-off."

**8. GHL-SPECIFIC SYNTHETIC ANECDOTES**
When the script calls for a "personal experience," generate anecdotes that reference real GHL pain points and workflows. These must be specific enough to feel lived-in:
- BAD (generic): "I once had a client who needed help with their CRM."
- GOOD (specific): "I had a pest control company in Phoenix — twelve technicians, no centralized booking. We set up the Go High Level calendar with round-robin assignment and within the first week their booking rate went from maybe forty percent to over eighty. The owner texted me a screenshot of his schedule and said 'what did you do to my phone.'"
- GOOD (troubleshooting): "I remember spending a solid two hours trying to figure out why my Twilio number wasn't sending texts. Turned out I had the wrong messaging service SID. One tiny field. Two hours."
- Always tie anecdotes back to the lesson. The story serves the instruction, never the other way around.

## CALIBRATION EXAMPLES: GOOD vs BAD

**--- BAD SCRIPT OPENING (every red flag) ---**

> "In today's video, we're going to dive into how to set up automated follow-ups in your CRM. Whether you're a beginner or an experienced marketer, this comprehensive guide will help you unlock the power of automation and take your business to the next level. Studies show that sixty-seven percent of businesses lose leads due to poor follow-up. So without further ado, let's get into it."

**What's wrong**: Cliché hook ("In today's video"), 5 banned phrases ("dive into", "without further ado", "unlock the power", "take it to the next level", "whether you're a beginner or experienced"), fake statistic (no source), no specificity, no personality, no open loop, no concrete detail, uniform sentence length, no contractions ("we are going to").

**--- GOOD SCRIPT OPENING (what we want) ---**

> "Last month I set up a follow-up sequence for a pest control company in Phoenix. Fourteen leads came in over the weekend. By Monday morning, eleven of them had already gotten three touchpoints — two texts and an email — and two had booked appointments. Nobody touched anything. The owner called me Monday morning and said... 'I thought you were personally responding to these.' I wasn't. I'm gonna show you exactly how I built that workflow in Go High Level, step by step. And honestly, the setup is way simpler than you'd think."

**What's right**: Specific story (pest control, Phoenix), real numbers (14 leads, 11 contacted, 2 booked), concrete outcome, dialogue that sounds real, open loop (how?), conversational tone ("I'm gonna", "way simpler"), varied rhythm (long → short → long → fragment → medium), zero clichés, Transformation Arc embedded (nightmare = manual follow-up, solution = workflow, quick win = booked appointments).

**--- BAD BODY SECTION ---**

> "GoHighLevel allows for Twilio integration, which enables users to send SMS messages automatically. This is an important feature for businesses that want to improve their communication workflows. The integration process involves several steps that we will cover in detail."

**What's wrong**: Passive, textbook tone. No contractions. Generic ("several steps"). Filler sentence ("this is an important feature"). No real menu paths. No personality.

**--- GOOD BODY SECTION ---**

> "OK so here's where we connect Twilio. Click on Settings — it's that little gear icon in the bottom left. Then Phone Numbers, then Twilio. Now I gotta warn you — this next screen trips people up all the time. You need your Account SID and your Auth Token. Not your API key. I made that mistake the first time and spent two hours wondering why nothing was sending. Your Account SID is right on your Twilio dashboard — it's the long string that starts with A-C. Copy that, paste it here. Auth Token is right below it — click the little eye icon to reveal it."

**What's right**: Real menu paths (Settings > Phone Numbers > Twilio), specific UI details (gear icon, bottom left), honest mistake ("I made that mistake"), specific technical detail (Account SID starts with AC, eye icon to reveal Auth Token), warning that prevents viewer frustration, conversational throughout.
